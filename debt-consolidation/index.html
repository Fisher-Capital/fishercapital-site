What's Usually Happening

Where debt consolidation files get complicated

Monthly obligations are harder to carry than they should be

Multiple payments across several creditors, each at a different rate, adds up fast. If equity exists in a property, there may be a path to simplifying those obligations — though it comes with its own costs and trade-offs.

The bank said no to a refinance

Banks assess LTV, income, and total debt servicing together. Adding debt to a mortgage increases the mortgage balance, which affects those ratios. If one metric is offside, the refinance gets declined even when equity is clearly there.

It's not obvious whether consolidating actually helps

Rolling high-interest debt into a mortgage lowers the rate on that portion — but extends repayment over 20+ years. The monthly payment goes down; the total cost of that debt often goes up. Whether the trade-off makes sense depends on the specific numbers.

What Tends to Exist

Consolidation paths — what each one involves

Which path is available depends on LTV, income, credit, and the current lender. These are the options that commonly come up — not all will apply to every file.

Conventional Refinance
If your file supports it — income, credit, and LTV within A-lender parameters — a refinance adds the debt to the mortgage balance at the mortgage rate. Requires an appraisal, legal fees, and in some cases a prepayment penalty if breaking mid-term. The rate on the consolidated portion drops significantly. Total cost of the refinanced debt over the amortization period increases.
B-Lender Refinance
If A-lender criteria don't support the refinance — because of credit history, income type, or LTV — some B-lenders will lend to higher LTV ratios with different qualifying criteria. The rate is higher than A, but the rate on the consolidated debt is still far below credit card rates. B-lender refinances also carry lender fees, which need to be part of the cost calculation.
HELOC Instead of Refinancing
If you're already with an A-lender and have equity, a HELOC (home equity line of credit) may accomplish the same goal without touching the mortgage. You draw on the line to pay off the high-interest debt, then carry the balance at the HELOC rate — typically prime plus a margin. Requires income and credit support to qualify, and may not be available if your situation has changed since the mortgage was set up.
Private Lending as a Bridge
If neither A nor B options are available immediately, private lending can sometimes bridge to a better position — consolidating urgently at higher short-term rates with the intent of refinancing into a regulated lender within 12–24 months as credit improves or income documentation strengthens. The cost is real. It makes sense only when the alternative is worse.

Some B-lenders lend to higher LTV ratios, but with higher rates and fees. The appraised value matters as much as how much equity you feel you have.

The real cost includes: legal fees (typically $1,000–$2,000+), an appraisal fee, any prepayment penalty if breaking mid-term, lender fees if using a B-lender, and — importantly — the total interest on the consolidated debt amortized over the remaining mortgage term. A $20,000 credit card balance consolidated into a 25-year amortization at mortgage rates costs more in total interest than paying off the credit card in 3 years, even at a higher rate. The monthly payment benefit is real; the total cost calculation is what most people don't run.
