What's Usually Happening

What a bank decline actually means

It happened fast, and without much explanation

Declines often come quickly. Banks are not required to explain the specific reason in detail, which leaves most people uncertain about what actually went wrong and whether it's fixable.

One metric didn't fit — the file wasn't necessarily unsalvageable

Banks run files through automated underwriting. A single ratio being offside — debt service, credit score, LTV — can trigger a decline even if everything else is solid. The decline doesn't tell you which one.

You're not sure what to try next

Another bank? An alternative lender? Wait and fix something first? The right next step depends on what caused the first decline. Without that information, it's hard to know whether the next attempt will go differently.

What Tends to Exist

After a decline, here's what the path forward usually looks like

The right path depends on what caused the decline. These are the options that commonly come up — none of them apply universally.

Understand the Decline First
Not all declines are the same. Credit score, income qualification, debt ratios, property type, employment type — each one changes what options exist. Understanding which factor was the issue is the starting point before looking at any lender.
Alternative Lenders Are Regulated
B-lenders operate under FSRA oversight. They're not payday lenders or private money — they're regulated financial institutions with real mortgage products. Their underwriting criteria are different from banks, not necessarily worse. Many files that fail at a chartered bank qualify at a B-lender on the same terms.
Credit Was the Issue
If credit was the factor, how old the issue is and what caused it matters more than the current score alone. Some lenders look at the trend over time. A discharged bankruptcy from 4 years ago is treated differently than a collection from 6 months ago, even if the scores are similar.
Income Was the Issue
If qualifying income was the problem, it's worth understanding whether it's a documentation issue or a structural one. Sometimes income exists but isn't being presented in a way the lender can use. Other times the income genuinely won't support the amount — and knowing that early avoids a worse outcome later.

After a decline — what people usually ask

The decline itself doesn't appear on your bureau. What does affect your score is the hard credit pull that happened when you applied. Each hard inquiry has a small, temporary impact. Multiple hard pulls in a short window (typically 14–45 days depending on the bureau) are often treated as a single inquiry for mortgage shopping purposes — but it's worth understanding where your credit stands before applying again.
