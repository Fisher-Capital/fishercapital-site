What's Usually Happening

Three renewal situations worth understanding

The renewal offer came and it doesn't look great

Your existing lender sent terms. Whether those are competitive depends on what's available at your current qualification profile. They know most people won't move — that's often reflected in the offer they send first.

Your lender isn't renewing you

This happens — credit events, changes in lender policy, or shifts in your file since the original application. A non-renewal creates urgency. Options narrow the closer you get to the maturity date. Addressing it early matters.

Your situation has changed since you first qualified

Income, employment, or credit may look different now than when you originally got the mortgage. Whether that affects your renewal options — and how — depends on where things currently stand and which lenders you're eligible with.

What Tends to Exist

Renewal options — what the paths look like

Which of these applies depends on your situation, your current lender, and the timing. These are the options that commonly come up.

Shopping the Renewal
At maturity, you can move lenders without a prepayment penalty. Shopping your renewal typically means a new full application — credit pull, income verification, property assessment. For most borrowers whose situation hasn't changed significantly, this is a straightforward process. The question is whether the rate differential justifies the effort.
Non-Renewal Situations
If the lender isn't renewing, the path forward depends on equity, credit, and income. Sufficient equity opens up B-lender and sometimes private options even when A-lender criteria aren't met. The further out from maturity you address this, the more options tend to be available — time creates flexibility.
Early Renewal or Break-and-Refi
If your term isn't up, breaking the mortgage triggers a prepayment penalty — typically the greater of 3 months' interest or the IRD (interest rate differential). Whether breaking early makes sense depends on the rate gap, remaining term, and the penalty calculation. Sometimes it pays off. Sometimes it doesn't. The math should come before the decision.
Improving the File Before Renewal
If renewal is months away, there may be time to improve credit, pay down a card balance, or restructure income documentation before the new application. What's worth doing depends on what the specific issue is — not all improvements move the needle equally with every lender.

Your existing lender may also allow early renewal — but that offer is typically not their most competitive, since they're not at risk of losing you yet.

Some lenders use automated valuation models for straightforward properties, which can reduce cost and time. An appraisal is a real cost but is often small relative to the rate savings over a 5-year term. It's worth factoring in but rarely the deciding issue.
Private lending exists as a short-term bridge. It's more expensive than any regulated lender option — both in rate and in fees — but it can prevent a forced sale when no other path is available in the immediate term. The intent is usually to use the private term to improve the file (credit, income, LTV) and move to a B or A lender at the next renewal. It's a last-resort option, not a first one.
Rate is one factor — prepayment privileges, mortgage features, and lender flexibility also vary. A slightly higher rate with better prepayment terms can be worth more over a 5-year term than a lower rate with restrictions. That said, if the numbers are genuinely similar and the switch involves legal and appraisal costs, staying may be the right call. The answer depends on the full comparison, not rate alone.
